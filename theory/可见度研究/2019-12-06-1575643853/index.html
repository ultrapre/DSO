The issues, and the problem:

Recent conversations on several web sites, and most notably here on Cloudy Nights, about the visibility of galaxies in small scopes (I use the word portable) has led me to research this issue further. Let me explain the issues:

Every observing guide seems to list a figure, Magnitude, for the deep-sky objects. This magnitude figure is generally the total integrated magnitude of the object. Picture reducing the object to the size of a star, or blowing up the image of a star to the size of the galaxy, and you have a rough approximation of the Total Integrated Magnitude (henceforth called TIM) of that extended object.

Many, but not all, observing guides also list Surface Brightness Magnitude (henceforth called SB) as an additional magnitude of the deep-sky objects in the lists. This can be calculated more than one way, but is usually expressed as the average brightness of each square arc-minute (1/3600 of a square degree) of the extended object. This figure is usually lower (though not always) than the TIM figure.

The issue for both of these magnitudes is where to cut off the edge of the deep-sky object. Longer time-exposures of most deep-sky objects just keep showing ever-increasing sizes for those objects. So an arbitrary cut-off point, the faintness isophote (contour line of equal brightness) is often set at magnitude 25.0 per square arc-second (very approximately mag.16.33 in sq. arc-min parlance). This is about the brightness edge cutoff easily seen on most of the photographic plates taken for magnitude studies at the professional observatories.

The relevance of these two measurements for we amateur observers is that the chosen cutoff is usually fainter than we can see, so we dont actually see all the surface area used for the calculation of the overall brightness of the galaxies in question. Accordingly, the TIM calculation OVERSTATES the brightness we see because it includes a lot of area we cant see, and the SB calculation UNDERSTATES the brightness of the object because it calculates the average brightness using a lot of faint area we cant see.

You can understand all that, and still be confused about which figure has the most relevance for the amateur observer.

Take M33, for example. At magnitude 5.7 (TIM), its visible to the naked eye in a dark site. Yet, its not easy in most scopes. A lot of beginners have trouble finding it. The SB figure tells whyits magnitude 14.1 per square arc-minute. Thats not bright, and though it should be within reach of most small scopes, it certainly wont stand out as spectacularly as, say, M32, the companion of M31 (which, although small, is outstandingly bright).

So, is TIM or SB more indicative of a visibility index figure we can use to discern the ease of seeing a particular galaxy? Or neither?

Lets explore further.

Because neither magnitude type is particularly useful for these galaxies.

Enter the Third Solution:

There is a list of galaxies used by a lot of professionals. It is the Reference Catalogue of Bright Galaxies, originally developed by Gerard de Vaucouleurs in the 1950s, and revised in 1991 to form the 3rd Reference Catalogue of Bright Galaxiesinformally called the RC3. Among other features of usefulness is the field in this large galaxy list called the m_e, which is a field that is only calculated for a small number (about 3000) of the brightest galaxies in the list.

The field, m_e is a magnitude field, and is calculated by taking the average surface brightness of only the brightest half of the galaxy (from the mean brightness to the peak).
This figure comes much closer to calculating surface brightness from the parts of galaxies seen in typical amateurs telescopes, so also might come closer to corresponding to a Visibility Index for galaxies typically viewed by most of us.

Lets see how well it does (using the RC3 figure for our sample galaxies):

1. M32 mag.10.1
2. M104 mag.11.6
3. M82 mag.12.0
4. M81 mag.12.4
5. NGC 253 mag.12.8
6. M31 mag.12.9
7. NGC 4565 mag.13.1
8. M110 mag.13.2
9. M74 mag.13.7
10. NGC 891 mag.14.6

How well did we do?

5 identical matches, 3 only one place off, and only 2 werent close.
Which ones werent close? M31 and M82. Hmmm. Could it be my subjective evaluation is wrong because Im using a large scope at a dark site? Is M82 really easier to see than M81? Is M31 really harder than the others?

I got out my 5 Maksutov and went to look at all of these in a fairly light-polluted placemy home in LA. I had to wait a while, but I finally got a night in which magnitude 4 stars were faintly visible, and the Moon was going to set early. I observed all night.

My neighborhood has no street lights, and house lighting is architecturally controlled, so lighting in the immediate vicinity of my scope was non-existent. But, this IS LA, where, in the direction of downtown, the sky can be yellow-orange on most nights, and plain blue if theres a little water vapor in the air.

I tried to view as many of these galaxies as possible. Several werent visible at all: NGC 4565, M110, M74, NGC 891, and NGC 253 (because it wasnt convenient to view). But I did verify, to my satisfaction, that M82 is, indeed, easier to view than M81 in the circumstances of use. The one galaxy that was definitely easier to view than the RC3 would indicate was M31.

But I only could see the core of the galaxy. All the visible parts fit within a 30 field of view. Instead of seeing the brightest 50%, I was probably only seeing the brightest 10%, which was quite noticeable in the scope.

Well, I couldnt reconcile the RC3 to my subjective visibility list completelyonly 9 of the 10 samples matched up.

So far, so good. How did the RC3 stack up when a larger selection was made? I went back to the RC3, imported the entire catalogue into Excel, sorted the list, and compared about a hundred galaxies with my observing notes. Though my observing notes arent ranked, did I comment in them that the top hundred galaxies (in the RC3) were all bright and detailed?

Unfortunately not. The 100 brightest galaxies, using the m_e figure from the RC3 only put 4 Messier galaxies in the 100 brightest galaxies: M94, M32, M77, and M105. The rest of the hundred brightest galaxies included some faint Index Catalog (IC) galaxies, and a host of very small galaxies with bright cores. Perhaps the ranking really IS indicative of the brightness of the brighter half of those galaxies, but too many of them are tiny faint galaxies with very bright nuclei. Though this column in the RC3 may represent the ease of detection for the galaxies cores, it does not truly describe the ease of seeing the galaxy itself. I dont view as successful any list that makes M94 the brightest Messier galaxy.

In conclusion, the RC3 has potential to describe successfully the average brightness of a lot of galaxies as seen in large scopes; but, as a Visibility Index, it does not work well. Despite its success at dealing with a select group of bright galaxies, it fails at predicting the visibility of galaxiesespecially the bright ones.

Creating an Index to Visibility for all galaxies for the amateur astronomer is the goal. Until someone comes up with a viable formula for that, I have some recommendations:

1. Look at the Total Integrated Magnitude for the object. If the galaxy is small, you neednt look any further. But if the galaxy is large, say 5 or larger, then..
2. Look at the Surface Brightness of the galaxy (or calculate it). Use that figure to decide whether the galaxys fainter regions will be visible in your scope. It will help to know what the limit of your scope isyou can gain that with experience of looking for galaxies and either finding or not finding them. Just be aware the positions of many galaxies in the literature are wrongespecially for the fainter NGC onesso dont trust what you read if the galaxy is not found. I recommend going to the data files on www.ngcic.org and looking up (or downloading) the corrected position to see if your not having found an object means either that you couldnt see it or were looking in the wrong place.
3. Use the m_e field of the RC3 to compare to the SB figure to see if theres a large discrepancy between the normal SB figure and the brightest 50%.
4. Look at a lot of galaxies. My Faint notation from the early 90s might rate a Bright designation today. Experience makes faint stuff more visible.
5. Take notes. Keep a note card handy with the following questions and answer them all in your notes. Eventually, youll not need the card. Until then:
Whats the shape? (Round/Oval/Lenticular/VeryExtended/odd)
Whats the brightness? (Very Bright/Bright/moderate/Faint/VeryFaint/UltraFaint)
Whats the size? (V.Lrg/Lrg/Average/Sml/V.Sml/VV.Sml)
Whats the orientation? (Face-on/Edge-On/Some degree in between)
What shape is the core?
Along what axis does the core lie?
What shape is the nucleus?
Are there superimposed stars?
How is the field? Rich/average/sparse
Are there nearby companions?
How is the edge definition? Diffuse/Sharp on one sidedescribe
Is the core a lot brighter than the outlying areas?
Are there any stand-out features or details? Describe.

Its been my experience that even rank novices can often see quite a few details if answering those questions.

In the long run, you wont need a visibility index. In the meantime, feel free to work on your own scale created from your own experiences. Let all the members of Cloudy Nights know what youve found. And know that some of us are still working on it. But the RC3 isnt it. Alas.

Don Pensack, Los Angeles.

Starman,

Another index could be:

galaxy area/RC3.

The larger the area and the lower the RC3 magnitude, the bigger the index. Or you can reverse and go with lower numbers. Don't worry about units, indices can be a real dog's breakfast of units if you want. Whatever works. You can also multiply or divide by 10 or 100 or whatever to get everyday nice numbers.

An alternate would be to square one of the numbers if it seems to need more emphasis. For example, try area/RC3^2 Again we don't care about units, just what works to model a complex situation.

Including galaxy area will help account for the difficulty in observing small galaxies.

The difference would be the difference between the 50% level in brightness and the average surface brightness of the brightest 50%.
An example would be a galaxy whose brightest square arc second is magnitude 15, making the mean magnitude 20. The average brightness of the brighter half might be magnitude 17.5 (with a bright core), making the difference +2.5 magnitudes.
In contrast, a galaxy with a peak of magnitude 18 might have a difference of +1.75, making it harder to see.

I think that one problem with this analysis is that you're using the term "ease of visibility" to mean many different things. That's the main reason why you got discrepant results from your top-ten list and your lots-of-galaxies list.

I was clued into this by your rating of the selected 10:

1. M31
2. M32
3. M104
4. M81
5. M82
6. NGC 253
7. NGC 4565
8. M110
9. M74
10. NGC 891

I can't imagine rating them in this order -- not through any instrument nor under any sky conditions. But my order would depend on the instrument and sky conditions. I'll leave NGC 253 off the list because it's sufficiently low at latitude 42N to skew the results, and rate the other 9. Here's the difficulty through my 10x30 binoculars under moderately dark skies:

1. M31
2. M81
3. M82
4. M110
5. M104
6. M32
NGC 4565 not sure if visible
M74 and NGC 891 definitely not visible

Here, the small size (or, if you prefer, high surface brightness) works against M32 and M104 because one of the limiting factors is the ability to distinguish the galaxies from stars at the low 10X magnification.

Using my 70mm refractor under the same conditions, the top six would probably all remain the same, followed by NGC 4565 and M74 in that order. I think that NGC 891 is beyond my ability in the 70mm scope.

Through my 7-inch Dob from urban skies (roughly mag 18.0 per square arcsec in the SQM), M110 would plunge in the "ease" rating:

1. M31
2. M81
3. M82
4. M32
5. M104
6. NGC 4565
7. M110
8. M74 extremely difficult
9. NGC 891 definitely not visible

What's going on here? I suggest that your initial ordering of the 10 selected galaxies was actually quite arbitrary. In fact, these galaxies are all showpieces through big scopes under dark skies. It's not meaningful to compare the "ease" of things that are completely effortless. So you were trying to extrapolate three quite different kinds of difficulty:

1. How hard would this be for an experienced observer under good skies using a very small telescope?
2. How hard would this be for an experienced observer under heavy light pollution using a biggish scope?
3. How hard would this be for someone who has never seen a galaxy before?

Question #1 could be explored quite rigorously by taking a 100mm refractor and masking it down until the galaxy in question becomes invisible. I suggest that the results would correlate *very* well with total integrated magnitude.

Question #2 is what you were trying to answer in L.A. You have to be extremely careful here, because different parts of the sky can be very different in an urban area. Sky brightness is typically 1 mag greater than the zenith at 30 degrees in the best direction and 2 mags greater at 30 degrees in the worst direction. If you look at Brian Skiff's data from Mars Hill in Flagstaff (which is indeed urban by any normal definition), you'll find an even greater skew between best and worst direction.

In any case, when skyglow is the limiting factor, that weights surface brightness *much* higher in the total equation.

As for inexperience being the limiting factor -- which, I think, is what you were trying to get at -- that probably also weights surface brightness more heavily. M33 can indeed be a little hard for beginners to see even under pretty good sky conditions, simply because it's not what they're expecting. But once they've aquired "galaxy eyes," it tends to become preposterously easy.

I guess the lesson here is that all beginners are not alike -- nor all experienced observers, either. And just as for experienced observers, ease of visibility for beginners is going to depend heavily on whether the limting factor is aperture or sky brightness.

Since the great majority of Americans live in suburbs, and beginners are unlikely to travel far to do their observing, it's a fair bet that sky brightness is a major factor for most beginners.

Tony is right. The ranking of visibility of galaxies will heavily depend on several factors.

If simply seeing that the galaxy is there is the measure, then peak brightness of the center will be the most important factor. M31 will top all lists because it is easy to the naked eye, normally, or to very small optical aid (like a 6x30 finder) otherwise.

If seeing extent, or size, of the galaxy is the criterion, then a shallow brightness gradient will be preferred, and this will make small, edge-on, galaxies appear near the top of a list.

The m'_e figure from the RC3 would appear to offer the best of all worlds, since it describes only the brightest half of each galaxy, and by providing an average of that half should directly tell us how bright, and hence, how visible, the brightest half is (an average will take into account the peak brightness as well). But the galaxies ranking, using this figure, did not correspond to anyone's list of the brightest objects, excluding from the top 100 nearly every galaxy universally considered bright.

As for Tony's ranking, to simulate the effects of light pollution, I viewed several of the galaxies in my list of 10 while still in twilight. I can find most of them in a second or two, so a nearly simultaneous comparison was easy.

I'm going to stick with my original ranking of 10. M104 was definitely seen earlier in twilight than M81 or M82, and while the core of M81 was seen early, the full size of M82 appeared long before M81's outer areas.

I haven't yet done the same for M110, but its low surface brightness isn't going to weigh in its favor. Its core would appear as a star when a definite lenticular shape would be seen in M104.

I ranked the 10 samples as I did based on my notes and based on memory, and based on the twilight viewing (to simulate light pollution). In my magnitude 17.5 skies in LA, I tried to view all these objects when past the meridian, as my West is darker than my East. Even then, I'm mystified at Tony's ranking of M32. It's easy in twilight through the 6x30 finder. Its peak brightness may be higher than M31, and is certainly higher than the others. Were it in a part of the sky away from M31, it's be considered super-easy.

But, however you rank those 10, the ranking from the m'_e field of the RC3 doesn't provide a reality-based ranking. It's as if NGC 891 was ranked above M31 to show the absurdity of the figure.

What I'm trying to create is an index number that will tell inexperienced observers how hard it will be to see a particular galaxy. We could disagree on a 1.5 or 1.6, while still agreeing that a 10, say, is a lot easier to see than 1.5. We don't have to agree on every galaxy to have an index that works.

What doesn't work, IMHO, is total integrated magnitude, the figure most often quoted in books. Nor does surface brightness (though I use this figure a lot to tell me how large I'll see the object).

Why even try? Reading the posts of beginners here on Cloudy Nights, and on other sites, convinces me that beginners have a hard time seeing certain objects. If I could provide a ranking of visibility, then they would know in advance that M51, say, was a lot harder to see than M81, and they wouldn't try to make M51 their first-viewed DSO. To the people working their way through the Herschel 400 or the NGC, this ranking wouldn't be particularly relevant. But it might save a lot of grief to a lot of beginners.

My favorite comparison is M33 versus M76. Granted, M76 isn't a galaxy, but the principle is the same. There is probably no one mathematical index that will accurately order the visibility of galaxies or other extended objects due to lack of data on dispersion of light within objects.

Magnitudes per square arc second (MPSAS) used in Clark's _Visual Astronomy of the Deep Sky_ is a reasonably good indicator. Clark lists the MPSAS for 616 common DSOs in Appendix E to the _Visual Astronomy_, that is available online at -

Active link:
Clark Appendix E

Text link:
http://www.clarkvisi...appendix-e.html .

But as his book notes MPSAS is imperfect because it does not capture the dispersion index for the DSOs. For any two galaxies with the same integrated magnitude, some have bright cores; others weaker cores and brighter disks.

In _Visual_, Clark develops his visibility model further, by adding the background brightness of the night sky to yield a contrast index (described in the footnotes to Appendix E). He then factors-in the reaction of the human eye and telescope performance to yield his Minimum Detection Magnitude (MDM) index.

For a rough pre-planning galaxy visibility index in my personal observing spreadsheet catalogue, I compute the MPSAS of the object, measure the MPSAS of the night sky using a Sky Quality Meter, and then have the spreadsheet compute a rough contrast index. Then I work down my list of the night's objects using a descreasing contrast index until I find the real limit for that night.

My experience using this technique is that if the contrast between the object and the night sky is slightly less than negative, it probably will not be visible. (This ignores the small aperture question, i.e. - less than 5 inches.)

The contrast index roughly captures two characteristics of the galaxy, where the integrated magnitude captures only one: the relationship between its (1) size and (2) brightness. Bright galaxies where average brightness is condensed into a small disk may be visible when a galaxy of the same integrated magnitude where the average brightenss is spread out over a larger area may not be visible.

This variant on Clark's methods suffers the same imprecision that MDM suffers: data in catalogues does not capture dispersion within the object. Some galaxies may have bright cores and little light in the disk and you can see the bright core with a small aperture. Other galaxies may have their light dispersed into the disk, have faint core and will not register in a small aperture.

Using the contrast index as a guide, I have problems correctly ordering the visibility of M57, M83 and M84. Some experience-based adjustments based on one's knowledge of the night sky are inevitable.

Dispersion within an object is just a built-in limitation of any catalogue-based galaxy visibility index. This applies equally to any integrated magnitude rule-of-thumb. The characteristic just isn't captured in the available catalogue data. Maybe when the data improves, the algorithms can be improved.

Clark's MDM or ODM index is built into some popular observing programs like Rodham's Astroplanner.

Active Link:

Astroplanner homepage

Astroplanner includes a full implementation of ODM algorithm that also considers the effect of aperture - for those of you using less than 5 inches of aperture. (In surfing the net today, I was unable to locate Mel Bartel's DOS implementation of Clark's ODM algorthim. Good thing I still that copy somewhere on the hard-drive. - )

The other half of the galaxy visibility index question is the applied aperture. Small scopes will not gather as much light and see as much as large scopes. m'_25 is:
"m'_25 = the mean surface brightness in magnitudes per square arcmin (B-mag/arcmin^2) within the mu_B = 25.0 B-mag/arcsec^2 elliptical isophote of major axis D_25 and axis ratio R_25, defined as in RC2 (Equation 21) by: m'_25 = B_T + Delta m_25 + 5 logD_25 - 2.5 logR_25 - - 5.26, where Delta m_25 = 2.5 log(L_T/L_D_25) = B_25 - B_T is the magnitude increment contributed by the outer regions of a galaxy fainter than mu_B = 25.0 B-mag/arcsec^2."
The way I read this is that this is the mean surface brightness is that of the galaxy (B Mag, that is) BRIGHTER than 25.

m'_e is:
"mean B-band surface brightness in magnitudes per square
arcmin (B-mag/arcmin^2) within the effective aperture A_e, and its mean error, as given by the relation m'_e = B_T + 0.75 + 5logA_e - 5.26. m'_e is statistically related to the effective mean surface brightness, mu'_e (RC2, p. 31; Olson and de Vaucouleurs 1981), with which it coincides when log R = 0 (i = 0 degrees) (Section 3.8.a, page 49)."
The way I read this is the m'_e is the surface brightness of the area above the brightness of the mean.

Here is Brian Skiff's message (responding to one of mine) wherein he defines these two fields in the RC3 and suggests a contrast figure can be derived from them:
http://groups.yahoo....o/message/17770

I have not done the math, but I am looking into it.

I have done extensive looking into Clark's figures, however and find some flaws:
1)He assumes the brightness of a dark sky is mag.24 per square arc-sec, yet study after study suggests that 22.0 is really the case. This would make everything suspect in his following calculations.
2)The ODM calculation would imply some objects have to be magnified much more than they really do to become visible. To wit, many of the objects in his list, from my own personal experience in the field, are viewable with lower magnifications and in smaller apertures than his list suggests. This is because he assumes a low transmission factor at the scope (0.7), lower than a lot of modern reflectors. Also because he already presumes a sky brightness of +2.0 magnitudes at 22.0, a perfect sky.
3)His list does not take into account genetic differences between observers, differences in extinction due to altitude (though, paradoxically, he has an appendix that tells you how to calculate it), coating reflectivity differences, or any other of a number of factors that would have to be taken into account to really come up with an ODM based on a contrast calculation.
4)Likewise, his contrast ratio discussion doesn't take into account seeing, or other important variables. He does briefly allude to how sky brightness would affect contrast ratio, but his figures are not based on instrument measurements.
5)Last, the mean surface brightness figures he uses do not take peak brightness into account. Two different galaxies with the same mean surface brightness figures could have different peak brightnesses, and, thus, different degrees of difficulty in detecting. Both would be treated equally in his argument.

This does not mean his work is not important, or has no merit. I think he is on the right track to figuring out why some objects are visible in some scopes and not others. With some extra variables thrown in, and some empirically derived examples, this could be turned into a much more valuable work.

In the meantime, I plan to investigate Skiff's suggestion for using the data from the RC3 to come up with a contrast figure that has relevance. I'm skeptical in advance, but have no data to consider, yet.
