Recently I started a thread about the different magnitude values given for M97 ( Here ) in different publications and we got into a discussions why different DSO’s are better visible than others. What I didn’t realize it that there is a good discussion of these issues in the ‘Observer Handbook 2005’ by the Royal Astronomical Society of Canada’ on pages 49-51. They are giving a good discussion how aperture, magnification, sky brightness, exit pupil etc. affect the visibility of an object. This is based on a paper by Blackwell in 1946, which I didn’t dig up yet, but the main results are summarized in this article in the Observer Handbook. To figure out the visibility requires a number of calculations and to ease these calculations I put together an Excel worksheet which does it for you. The problem was to find a formula that approximates & interpolates the visibility thresholds determined empirically by Blackwell (by having a number of people look at different object through a telescope at different magnifications). I think I got fairly close, at least within 0.2 magnitudes. I will try to improve on that. In the meantime it would be great if you try this out and let me know if it works.

How to use it:

The values in the left column (in red) are the inputs you have to enter. These include the total magnitude of your object, the size of the object in two directions (in minutes), the telescope parameters (aperture, focal length, eyepiece focal length), and the sky brightness in mag/sq second. As a guide a suburban sky might be 18.5 mag/sq’’, sites with 5th magnitude stars visible are about 19.5, dark country sites are 21, and the best you can get anywhere on earth is about 22.
The excel worksheet will then calculate different parameters for you (in blue – do not enter anything into these fields, it will overwrite the formulas). The most important is what I call ‘Contrast’. This parameter is the difference of the adjusted magnitude per square second of your object and the empirically determined threshold magnitude at which 50% of people would detect such an object under the particular sky conditions. The brightness of the sky of course also depends on your eyepiece & telescope. All that is taken into account in this worksheet.

The file can be used several ways:
1) Look at some objects and determine which object you can barely detect. This way you can figure out how dark your sky is. Adjust the sky brightness until you get just a small positive contrast value for an object you can barely find. That should get you pretty close.
2) Once you know your sky brightness you can use the file to plan your viewing session. Just type in the parameters of your objects and see which one should be visible and which ones will be difficult (negative contrast values).
3) Change the eyepiece values to see how much magnification you can use before losing the object & figure out the best eyepiece for observing.

Example: From the fact that I can barely find M95 with my Starblast, I figured out that I am observing from about a 20.5 mg/sq’’ sky. Now if I compare M97 to M95, I can find M97 much easier than M95, although M97 has a magnitude of 9.9 (supposedly) and M95 is 9.7 (i.e. supposedly brighter). However, M95 is more extended. Putting the values (magnitude & size) into the excel sheet I get a contrast value of 0.95 for M97, but only 0.25 for M95, which matches my observations. Another example is M108 which I initially had trouble finding, but have better luck than with M95. It has a contrast value of 0.37 for my telescope & sky, again consistent with my observations.

Try it out and let me know if it works. Thanks!

For links to all the articles by Blackwell, Clark, Schaefer, Bartels and Carlin related to visual detection and the further analysis articles written more recently see this post
LINKS to Web - Binocular and Astro Info in the binocular forum.

You will need to refer to articles in these sections of my post
Technical Reports - Optics - Vision
SEEING - Visual Detection
Limiting Magnitude

In the papers referred to above, I think you will want to review the information on Visual Optimum Detection Magnification, VODM by Mel Bartels and Optimum Magnified Visual Angle, OMVA by Roger Clark.

Also, pay special attention to Bradley E. Schaefer: Telescopic Limiting Magnitudes. This particular value varies from night to night and also from one area of the sky to another. Anyone trying to determine tyhe darkness rating for their sky must be aware, this value changes constantly and must always be determined on the night of observation, at the time of observation and in the area of observation.

The sky brightness figures derived from the calculator JUST made the object visible by .01 or .02 magnitude. The calculated sky brightness is lower still if a more reasonable contrast ratio of 0.5 magnitude is selected.

I probably have 100's more examples where the calculator is off by up to 3 magnitudes or more.

Some factors at work that influence what is seen:
--aerosol content of air (dryness)
--proximity of item to zenith
--dark adaptation of viewer(some people accuse me of being an owl)
--altitude of the site
--viewers experience
--cleanliness of scope and eyepiece(s)
--quality of seeing
--age of viewer(I was in my 40s when these were made)
--Fatigue
--magnification (I tend to observe at lower powers--say ~100X)
--wind (which vibrates and moves the scope)
And many others you can come up with.
All should be taken into account by your calculator.

Is this an impossible task? Probably not. But formulas give distinct figures that will be very different for different scopes, viewers, and conditions. So a healthy "error bar" needs to be added to any figure the calculator derives.

Hope this helps.

Pete,
Surface brightness per unit area will be more indicative of visibility. Some reference books list calculated surface brightnesses, usually in magnitude per square arc-minute.
But can you trust those figures? In many cases, an object gets several times larger if the limiting magnitude of its size measurement is set lower. At a magnitude 25 per square arc-second isophote, a galaxy might be several times larger than at a magnitude 22 isophote. A surface brightness figure calculated from the first dimension will be fainter than one calculated from the second. The second figure will likely be more relevant to the visual observer who cannot see the mag.25 isophote. So the surface brightness of large extended objects will usually read too low, while the surface brightness figures for a small object may be right on. But galaxies are often brightest in the center and fade toward the edges. So a galaxy with an average surface brightness of 14 may be easy to see, while a planetary with the same surface brightness may not be.
Any formula that purports to tell you IF you can see something in a particular scope will have to take into account:
Age of viewer
Experience level
Acuity of observer's vision
Fatigue level
Transparency
Size of scope
Type of scope
Driven or not?
Smoothness of drive motor
Type of coatings
Type of eyepiece (coatings, # of lenses, etc)
Wavefront accuracy from mirrors/lenses
Cleanliness of optics
Seeing
Atmospheric aerosol content
altitude
magnification
size of object
night sky brightness
Zenithal limiting magnitude
altitude of object above horizon (atmospheric extinction)
brightness gradient in object
predominant color of light transmission in object
whether filters are used
what kind of filter(s) are used

Good luck with your spreadsheet.

Since magnitude is 2.5 log brightness, you would have to add 2.5 log 3600 = 8.89 to convert from mag/sq min to mag per sq sec. So for example M97 has 21 mag/sq sec, and 12.1 mag/sq min.

I calculate surface brightness (mag/sq sec) by using S = m + 2.5 log (2827 x a x b) where m is the total magnitude and a & b are the major & minor diameters of the object in minutes. The 2827 factor compensates for the fact that very few DSO's--particularly galaxies--are rectangular in shape.

If they were rectangles, then you could simply multiply the maximum and minumum dimensions (a*b) to get an area. If those dimensions are expressed in arcminutes, you could multiply the product by 3600 to convert the surface area to square arcseconds.

However, most galaxies are elliptical in shape. The area of an ellipse is (pi*a*b)/4, where a and b are the maximum and minimum dimmensions, respectively. If you factor out pi/4, the product is 0.7854. Multiply 3600 by 0.7854 and you get 2827.4. The tenths can be rounded off.

As was pointed out to me by Lawrence Sayre, the contrast ratio of a deep-sky object is obtained by summing the brightness of the night sky with the brightness of the object and then dividing that by the brightness of the night sky.
This is why we can see objects whose brightness is below the brightness of the night sky.
